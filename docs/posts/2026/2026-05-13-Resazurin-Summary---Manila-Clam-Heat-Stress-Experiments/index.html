INTRO

As part of the resazurin assay development process, we conducted a series of experiments to evaluate the effects of heat stress on metabolic activity in two different groups of Manila clams (Ruditapes philippinarum, BLUE and TWEED coloration) which had been bred for low salinity tolerance (CONTROLS and SELECTED). This document provides a summary of the experimental design, methods, results, and conclusions from these experiments.

A variety of heat stress experiments were conducted on the clams, with the goal of assessing metabolic activity using the resazurin assay. The experiments included:

  • Experiment 1: Acute heat stress (exposure to 40°C for 2 hours)
  • Experiment 2: Recovery from heat stress (exposure to 40°C for 3.5 hours followed by overnight recovery in seawater at 10°C, followed by a second heat stress at 36°C for 4 hours)
  • Experiment 3: Acute heat stress (exposure to 36°C for 4 hours).

MATERIALS & METHODS

Clam measurements

Clams were photographed in their respective plates from below (i.e. reversed). Shell area was measured using ImageJ using the following steps:

  1. Open the image in ImageJ.
  2. Convert the image to 8-bit grayscale (Image > Type > 8-bit).
  3. Transform the image to correct orientation by flipping horizontally (Image > Transform > Flip Horizontally).
  4. Set the scale by drawing a line between “dots” to a known distance (10mm) (Analyze > Set Scale).
  5. Adjust the threshold to isolate the clam shells from background (Image > Adjust > Threshold > Apply).
  6. Fill holes in the thresholded image to ensure the entire shell area is captured (Process > Binary > Fill Holes).
  7. Analyze particles to measure the area of each clam shell (Analyze > Analyze Particles).

ImageJ images were saved, and the area measurements were exported to a CSV file for further analysis.

Experiment 1: Acute Heat Stress @ 40°C for 2 hours

Control clams (C) compared with selected clams (S) at 40oC.

Two plates per treatment were measured: “blue” and “red”, representing the “BLUE” and “TWEED” coloration of the clams.

Clams were processed in 24-well plates and submerged in 2.2mL of resazurin working solution; prepared by Sam White 20260330:

  • 986.66 mL filtered seawater (4oC filtered sea water from 11/19/2025 by AH)
  • 2.22 mL resazurin stock solution (from 7/20/2025 by VV)
  • 1.00 mL DMSO
  • 10.00 mL antibiotic solution (100x Penn/Strep & 100x Fungizone)

Plates were measured every 30mins on a Synergy HTX (Agilent) plate reader.

Note

Filtered sea water had been stored at 4oC, so working resazurin solution was cold when introduced to the clams on both days.

Experiment 2: Recovery from Heat Stress

Control clams (C) compared with selected clams (S) at 36oC.

Clams were processed in 12-well plates and submerged in 5.0mL of resazurin working solution.

Plates were measured every 30mins on a Synergy HTX (Agilent) plate reader.

Plate layout was randomized.

After 3.5hrs, the clams were returned to seawater at 10oC for overnight recovery, and then subjected to a second heat stress/resazurin assay at 36oC for 4hrs the following day.

RESULTS

Analyses below are based on the area under the curve (AUC) of fluorescence over time for each well, which serves as a proxy for metabolic activity. AUC was calculated using the trapezoidal rule, and statistical analyses were performed to compare the control and selected groups under each experimental condition (Huffmyer et al. 2025).

Experiment 1: Acute Heat Stress @ 40°C for 2 hours

Statistically significant differences in AUC were observed in the following comparisons:

  • SELECTED BLUE vs SELECTED TWEED (p_adj = 0.005): SELECTED BLUE clams had higher AUC than SELECTED TWEED clams, suggesting that the SELECTED BLUE clams may have had higher metabolic activity under acute heat stress compared to SELECTED TWEED clams.

  • TWEED CONTROL vs TWEED SELECTED (p_adj = 0.021): TWEED CONTROL clams had higher AUC than TWEED SELECTED clams, suggesting that the SELECTED TWEED clams may have had lower metabolic activity under acute heat stress compared to TWEED CONTROL clams.

Experiment 2: Recovery from Heat Stress

Day 1: Heat Stress at 36°C for 3.5hrs

Statistically significant differences in AUC were observed in the following comparisons:

  • CONTROL BLUE vs SELECTED BLUE (p_adj = 0.050): CONTROL BLUE clams had higher AUC than SELECTED BLUE clams, suggesting that the CONTROL BLUE clams may have had higher metabolic activity under acute heat stress compared to SELECTED BLUE clams.

  • CONTROL BLUE vs SELECTED TWEED (p_adj = 0.050): BLUE CONTROL clams had higher AUC than TWEED SELECTED clams, suggesting that the SELECTED TWEED clams may have had lower metabolic activity under acute heat stress compared to TWEED CONTROL clams.

Statistically significant differences in AUC were observed in the following comparisons:

  • BLUE CONTROL vs SELECTED BLUE (p_adj = 0.005): BLUE CONTROL clams had higher AUC than SELECTED BLUE clams, suggesting that the CONTROL BLUE clams may have had higher metabolic activity under acute heat stress compared to SELECTED BLUE clams.

  • TWEED CONTROL vs SELECTED TWEED (p_adj = 0.021): TWEED CONTROL clams had higher AUC than SELECTED TWEED clams, suggesting that the SELECTED TWEED clams may have had lower metabolic activity under acute heat stress compared to TWEED CONTROL clams.

Day 2: Heat Stress at 36°C for 4hrs

There were no significant differences in AUC between any comparisons on day 2, suggesting that the overnight recovery period may have mitigated the effects of heat stress on metabolic activity.

Experiment 3: Acute Heat Stress @ 36°C for 4 hours

Statistically significant differences in AUC were observed in the following comparisons:

  • CONTROL BLUE vs CONTROL TWEED (p_adj = 0.050): CONTROL BLUE clams had higher AUC than CONTROL TWEED clams, suggesting that the CONTROL BLUE clams may have had higher metabolic activity under acute heat stress compared to CONTROL TWEED clams.

  • CONTROL BLUE vs SELECTED BLUE (p_adj = 0.050): CONTROL BLUE clams had higher AUC than SELECTED BLUE clams, suggesting that the CONTROL BLUE clams may have had higher metabolic activity under acute heat stress compared to SELECTED BLUE clams.

Important

Volume loss due to clam spitting may have affected the results, as those wells which experienced volume loss were excluded from the analysis. This may have reduced the sample size and statistical power of the comparisons.

SUMMARY

Experiment 1 (40°C, 2 hours): At the highest acute temperature, coloration- and selection-specific effects were evident. SELECTED BLUE clams showed higher AUC than SELECTED TWEED, and within the TWEED coloration controls had higher AUC than selected individuals. These results indicate temperature-dependent responses where both coloration and selection status influenced metabolic activity at 40°C.

Experiment 2 (Recovery trial; initial 36°C exposure and post-recovery 36°C exposure): On Day 1 (36°C, 3.5 hours) CONTROL BLUE clams exhibited higher AUC than SELECTED BLUE and SELECTED TWEED, suggesting greater metabolic activity during the initial 36°C exposure. After overnight recovery, Day 2 (36°C, 4 hours) showed no significant differences across comparisons, consistent with the idea that recovery attenuated the measurable heat-stress effects seen on Day 1.

Experiment 3 (36°C, 4 hours): In this independent 36°C acute exposure CONTROL BLUE clams again tended to show higher AUC (higher than CONTROL TWEED and SELECTED BLUE). Note that several wells experienced volume loss and were excluded from analysis; coupled with some plate temperature heterogeneity, this likely reduced sample size and statistical power for some comparisons.

Similarities: Across trials, coloration and selection status repeatedly modulated AUC-based metabolic responses, and CONTROL BLUE clams frequently showed relatively higher metabolic activity during 36°C exposures (Experiment 2 Day 1 and Experiment 3). This recurring pattern suggests a consistent biological tendency for CONTROL BLUE animals to respond more strongly under acute 36°C stress compared with some other groups.

Differences: The 40°C exposure (Experiment 1) produced a distinct pattern (notably SELECTED BLUE > SELECTED TWEED and TWEED CONTROL > TWEED SELECTED) that does not map directly onto the 36°C results, indicating temperature-dependent shifts in which groups are most responsive. Recovery (Experiment 2 Day 2) eliminated differences observed on Day 1, highlighting that prior stress and recovery state strongly affect measurable metabolic activity. Experimental confounders (cold resazurin working solution, uneven heating, and volume loss) may also contribute to inter-experiment differences and should be considered when interpreting effect sizes.
